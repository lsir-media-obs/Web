Mapping the News Landscape

The Influence of Ownership on Media Coverage





OUR MISSION

We are researchers from EPFL, fighting for media accountability and information democracy. We work to create an exhaustive map of the digital media ecosystem and to make it accessible to the public. In short,

  • The independance of news channels acquired by large broadcasting groups has recently been questioned.
  • We developped an algorithm that highlights the influence of ownership on broadcasted news and highlight changes in content diffusion.
  • Our method was able to map the evolution of the media landscape in the last 3 years, based on 500M articles from around 8K sources.

THE PROBLEM

The digitalization and globalization of our news channels had a profound impact on worldwide information diffusion. Today, information is processed in mass by large organizational structures, before being propagated to local channels. This process often obfuscates the origin of a news and reduces its traceability. More worryingly, large broadcasting groups have recently been suspected of massive consolidation of resources, meaning that multiple local channel transmit the same unilateral selection of news. This worrying lack of diversity has recently been exposed by John Oliver in his show Last Week Tonight (HBO). Soon after, a video exemplifying the problem was posted online. We developped an algorithm to automate the tracking and the monitoring of broadcasted news content. One of the main difficulty in the development of such method is the absence of ground thruth : any selection of events is partial by nature and is only comparable to the selection made by other channels. Therefore, our method evaluates the similarity across news entities and projects each of the considered channel on a comprehensive map, as show in see Fig.1. Since the structure arises directly from the coverage we can extract factors of the bias, such as geographic relationships, thematic regards or higher-order structures, despite them not always being evident to the inexperienced eye. One notable example is the presence of small, local broadcast affiliates, usually household names (e.g. Fox or ABC). However, many of these outlets are actually owned by larger corporate structures, some of which, most infamously Sinclair Broadcast Group, have been accused of imposing ideologically charged content on these downstream channels, unbeknownst to the casual viewer.

Our method borrows from the field of personalization with a set of techniques commonly used in recommender systems, such as those used on Amazon or Netflix. In particular, the algorithm predicts the probability of a channel to cover a specific event, knowing which other sources have selected it. To this extent, the algorithm learns a fingerprints of the sources' coverage patterns. We can then compare these coverage profiles to uncover similarities in the choice of their reporting, usually indicating the presence of a common bias. Sources appearing close together on the map are likely to have very similar selection of events.

An EVOLVING MAP

The selection of reported events can generate a comprehensive map of the the news ecosystem. However, the methods only provide a static view into this inherently dynamic system. In order to circumvent this limitation, we developped an additional technique that models the evolution of the media landscape. The static snapshot becomes an evolving map that highlights important changes that occur when channels modify they news coverage habits. We demonstrate the potential of the method for news monitoring applications and investigative journalism by shedding light on important changes in programming induced by mergers and acquisitions, policy changes, or network-wide content diffusion. An example of the phenomenon is shown in Fig.2 : news sources tend to agglomerate themselves into cohesive clusters after their acquisitions by three well-known news companies, clearly showing the consolidation of resources taking place in background.

DETECTING HIGHLY INFLUENCIAL BROADCAST GROUPS

One of the most common observable pattern is the convergence of a group of sources towards a specific position : as the similarity of their selection of news rises, the model will group the source together in space. We propose an automated method to detect this phenomenon by introducing the notion of attractors : the algorithm identifies regions of the map toward which many sources tend to cluster together. The region with high potential of attration are show in blue in Fig.3.

Once identified, the news sources moving toward those positions can be monitored. For example, the acquisition campaing performed by Sinclair can be visualized on the right figure. The distance from four sources to the attractor is abruptly reduced, suggesting an alignement of content post-acquisition.
